Understanding Pylon Signs

What is a Pylon Sign?

A pylon sign (also called a pole sign or freestanding sign) is a tall, elevated sign structure mounted on one or two poles, designed to maximize visibility from roadways and highways. These towering signs are essential for businesses that need to attract attention from passing traffic, particularly those set back from the road or competing with surrounding buildings and signage.

Pylon signs differ from monument signs primarily in height—while monuments typically stand under 8 feet, pylon signs can reach 50 feet or higher depending on local zoning regulations. This elevation makes them ideal for highway-adjacent businesses, shopping centers, and commercial developments where ground-level signage would be obscured.

Pylon Sign Components

A quality pylon sign consists of several key components that work together for durability and visual impact:

  • Foundation: Engineered concrete footings designed to support the structure and withstand wind loads. Foundation requirements are calculated based on local soil conditions and wind speed requirements per International Building Code (IBC) standards.
  • Support Structure: Steel poles or columns, typically galvanized and powder-coated for corrosion resistance. Single-pole and dual-pole configurations are available based on sign size and design requirements.
  • Sign Cabinet: The illuminated display housing, constructed from aluminum with polycarbonate or acrylic faces. Cabinet signs can be internally illuminated with LED modules for energy-efficient 24/7 visibility.
  • Electrical System: LED lighting systems have become the industry standard, offering 50,000+ hour lifespans and up to 80% energy savings compared to fluorescent or neon alternatives. All electrical work must meet NEC (National Electrical Code) requirements.

Zoning & Permit Requirements

Pylon sign installation requires careful attention to local zoning ordinances, which regulate maximum height, setback distances, illumination levels, and digital content restrictions. In Pennsylvania, the PA Department of Community & Economic Development provides guidance on municipal sign regulations, while PennDOT oversees signage along state highways.

Common permit requirements include:

  • Zoning permit or variance application
  • Structural engineering drawings stamped by a licensed PE
  • Electrical permit for illuminated signs
  • Landlord authorization (for leased properties)
  • Highway occupancy permit (if within PennDOT right-of-way)

ROI & Advertising Value

According to research from the International Sign Association (ISA), on-premise signage is one of the most cost-effective forms of advertising, with pylon signs delivering exceptional value for businesses on busy roadways. A well-positioned pylon sign can generate 50,000+ daily impressions at a fraction of the cost-per-impression of billboards, television, or digital advertising.

The U.S. Small Business Administration notes that effective signage is often a small business's single most important marketing investment, particularly for retail and service businesses that depend on drive-by traffic.
